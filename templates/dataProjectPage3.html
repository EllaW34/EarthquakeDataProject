Impacts of the Earthquakes


The impact of earthquakes are measured in terms of their gap, magnitude and significance.

The gap of an earthquake is its azimuthal gap measured in degrees. An Azimuth is the horizontal angle from a cardinal direction, most commonly north. Gaps with a measure greater than 180 degrees typically have a larger location and an uncertain depth. The range of the gaps for the earthquakes measured between {{miniGap}} and {{maxiGap}} degrees with {{avgGap}} degrees being the average.

The magnitude of an earthquake is a measure of its size at the source, which is determined by the amplitude of the seismic waves. Each unit increase in magnitude is measured as an increase in the total energy by a factor of about 32. A magnitude of -1 is thought of as very tiny, while 10 is thought of as incredibly powerful. The magnitude for each earthquake ranged from {{miniMag}} to {{maxiMag}} with {{avgMag}} being the average.

The significance of an earthquake is determined by many factors, including magnitude, maximum MMI (shaking intensity along with effects on people, objects and buildings), felt reports (reports made by people who felt an earthquake in order to measure the extent of the damage), and estimated impact. The significance is typically measured on a scale from 0 to 1,000. The range of the significance for each earthquake measured between {{miniSig}} and {{maxiSig}} with {{avgSig}} being the average.
